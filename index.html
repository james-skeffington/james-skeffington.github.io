Claude-Joseph Vernet

Claude-Joseph Vernet was an 18th-century French painter who is primarily known for his exquisite marine and landscape paintings, particularly his striking and detailed portrayals of seascapes, storms, and port scenes.

Three notable paintings by Claude-Joseph Vernet are "A Storm on a Mediterranean Coast" (1767), "The Shipwreck" (1772), and "View of the Port of Rochefort" (1762).

Claude-Joseph Vernet's contemporaries included notable artists such as Jean-Honoré Fragonard (1732-1806), François Boucher (1703-1770), and Maurice Quentin de La Tour (1704-1788).

The Enlightenment

Claude-Joseph Vernet painted "Moonlight" in 1772 while residing in France.

The 1770s in France were marked by the late Enlightenment, where intellectual and cultural growth coexisted with increasing economic strain and social inequality, setting the stage for the revolutionary upheaval that followed.

At that time, art was characterized by a transition from the ornate Rococo style to the more restrained and classical Neoclassical movement, reflecting the broader intellectual and cultural shifts of the Enlightenment era.

Nocturnal Maritime

"Moonlight" depicts a maritime scene, illuminated by the bright light of a full moon.

Ships rest on the calm waters while two groups of sailors pull their small wooden boats ashore.

In the foreground, a group of people huddle by the fire: one man smokes his pipe while another tends to the fire; the women each have baskets; a dog barks at the sailors.

Rococo & Neoclassicism

Claude-Joseph Vernet's painting 'Moonlight' is characterized by a style that aligns with the transition from Rococo to Neoclassicism, which was common in the later part of the 18th century. His work would typically exhibit the romantic and atmospheric qualities associated with Rococo, alongside a growing emphasis on the realism and subdued color palette that would come to define Neoclassicism. This blend allows for a dramatic yet precise portrayal of nature, particularly in his marine and landscape scenes.

Vernet & Turner

Vernet wasn't the only artist to depict nocturnal maritime scenes; Joseph Mallord William Turner's "Keelmen Heaving in Coals by Moonlight" (1835) (located nearby, in Gallery 57) provides a nice contrast to Vernet's painting.

Turner's piece exemplifies the Romantic era's focus on dynamic, dramatic scenes, emphasizing the interplay of human activity and the sublime power of nature, particularly evident in its vivid portrayal of industrial labor against the backdrop of moonlit waters.

In contrast, Vernet's "Moonlight" (1772) reflects the transitional phase between Rococo and Neoclassicism, focusing more on serene, harmonious maritime scenes with meticulous attention to atmospheric detail and a more subdued, orderly depiction of the natural world.
